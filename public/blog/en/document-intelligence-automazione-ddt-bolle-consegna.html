The hidden problem of operational documents

Ask any operations manager, warehouse worker or accounts-payable clerk at an SME how much time they spend each week handling delivery notes. The answer, almost invariably, is surprising: hours. Sometimes dozens of hours a month, between receiving, digitizing, checking, matching to orders, correcting errors and archiving.

The transport document — the delivery note — is one of the most common documents in the life of a company that buys, sells or moves goods. It's also one of the most neglected when it comes to automation. While electronic invoicing went through a regulatory revolution that forced digitization, delivery notes have often remained in the analog era: paper sheets, unstructured PDFs, emails with poorly named attachments, data entered by hand into the management system.

The result is predictable: transcription errors, missed matches with purchase orders, longer accounting close times, and a significant share of human work absorbed by activities that add no value [1]. Document Intelligence is the technological answer to this problem — mature, accessible and already adopted by companies of every size.

What Document Intelligence is

Document Intelligence is the ability of a software system to read, interpret and structure the content of unstructured documents — PDFs, images, scans — automatically extracting the relevant fields and making them available to company systems.

This is not the old OCR that "read" text without understanding it. Modern Document Intelligence systems combine optical character recognition with artificial-intelligence models trained to understand the semantic structure of a document: it knows that the number next to the words "Note number" is the document reference, not a quantity; it knows that "sender" and "recipient" are different entities even if they appear in variable positions depending on the supplier.

The role of composed models

A key element in the most effective implementations is the use of composed models: instead of training a single generic model, you create specialized models for each document type (or even for each supplier) and combine them into a single access point. The system receives the document, identifies which type it is, and routes the analysis to the most appropriate model [2].

This approach guarantees far higher accuracy than generalist models, especially when working with documents that have proprietary layouts or industry-specific terminology.

How it works: from PDF to structured data

The end-to-end process of a Document Intelligence solution for delivery notes typically unfolds in four phases, each of which contributes to the quality of the final result.

  1. Document intake: the delivery note enters the system through one of the supported channels — manual upload from a web interface, monitoring of a dedicated email inbox, direct integration with the management system or supplier portal. The system accepts PDFs, images and scans, even of imperfect quality.
  2. Analysis with the AI model: the document is sent to the recognition engine (such as Microsoft's Document Intelligence), which applies the most suitable composed model. The model extracts the configured fields: note number, date, sender, recipient, line items (item code, description, quantity, unit of measure), carrier, packages, weight. The results also include a confidence score for each extracted field.
  3. Validation and completion: the system automatically checks that mandatory fields are present and that the values make sense (dates in the correct format, positive numeric quantities, item codes existing in the master data). Documents with all fields complete are marked "ready for verification"; those with missing fields or anomalies are flagged for human review.
  4. Verification and transfer: an operator reviews the flagged documents, completes or corrects the necessary data, and validates the document. Once verified, the delivery note is transferred to the management system or ERP with all structured fields, ready for matching with the purchase order and accounting entry.

The key isn't to eliminate human intervention, but to focus it where it really matters: 70-80% of documents typically require no manual touch, while the remaining 20-30% are flagged precisely for operator review.

The verification flow: the machine helps, the professional decides

One of the elements that distinguishes serious implementations from superficial ones is the presence of a structured verification flow. It's not enough for the system to "read" the document: you need a clear process that guarantees data quality before it's fed into company systems.

A robust approach uses a well-defined state machine, in which each document goes through sequential phases with controlled transitions:

  • Processing: the document is awaiting analysis or being processed by the AI engine.
  • Incomplete: the analysis produced results, but mandatory fields are missing or the extracted values show anomalies requiring human intervention.
  • To verify: all mandatory fields are present; the document awaits review and confirmation by an operator.
  • Verified: an operator has confirmed the data is correct. The document is ready for transfer to the downstream system.
  • Transferred: the data has been sent to the management system or ERP. Final state, tracked with a timestamp and a reference to the user who carried out the transfer.
  • Error: the document can't be processed (corrupted file, unsupported format, insufficient quality). It's flagged for manual handling with the option to retry.

Every transition is recorded in the document's history, creating a complete audit trail: who did what, when, with what outcome. This traceability is fundamental for internal compliance and exception management [3].

What changes for companies that adopt it

The benefits of Document Intelligence are not only quantitative — fewer hours spent, fewer errors — but they structurally change the way a company manages its document flows.

Speed and predictability

Documents that arrive in the evening are processed overnight. On Monday morning the warehouse and accounting find everything already processed, verified or flagged, without the traditional return-to-work backlog. Cycle times shorten structurally; they no longer depend on people's availability.

Data quality in the management system

Manual entry inevitably introduces transcription errors: transposed figures, truncated descriptions, wrong item codes. A Document Intelligence system, once configured correctly, is deterministic: it always extracts the same fields with the same logic, and its errors — when they occur — are visible and traceable through confidence indicators.

Scalability without marginal costs

In a growing company, the volume of documents to handle increases in proportion to revenue. With manual processes, this means hiring additional staff. With a Document Intelligence system, processing capacity scales automatically with no significant marginal cost [4].

Freeing up time for value-added activities

Operators taken away from mechanical typing can be redirected toward activities that require judgment: exception handling, supplier relationships, analysis of discrepancies between what was ordered and delivered. The added value of human work increases, it doesn't decrease.

Integration with existing systems

A frequent concern when evaluating a Document Intelligence solution is integration with the systems already in use — ERP, management systems, supplier portals. It's a legitimate concern, but often overestimated.

Modern solutions offer several integration mechanisms, suited to very different technical contexts:

  • REST APIs: the system exposes standard endpoints for uploading documents, checking status, retrieving extracted data and transferring to external systems. Any modern or custom management system can integrate via API.
  • CSV/Excel export: for companies with legacy systems or spreadsheet-based workflows, periodic export of processed data is an immediate option that requires no development.
  • Webhooks: the system can actively notify downstream systems whenever a document changes state, enabling event-driven integrations without continuous polling.
  • Email integration: sending delivery notes via email to a dedicated address can be the documents' entry point, without requiring any change to suppliers' processes.

The guiding principle is adaptability: the solution must adapt to existing processes, not the other way around. A well-designed implementation has minimal impact on operational routines during the adoption phase.

When it's a fit and when it isn't

Document Intelligence isn't the right solution for every situation. It's useful to understand in advance when the ROI is solid and when, instead, the investment risks not being justified.

When it makes sense to adopt it

  • High document volume: automation becomes worthwhile from a few dozen documents a month; above a few hundred, the advantage is clear.
  • Documents with a recurring structure: if your suppliers' delivery notes always have the same fields in roughly the same positions, the model trains quickly and reaches high accuracy.
  • Costly or frequent errors: if transcription errors cause discrepancies that are hard to reconcile, returns, disputes with suppliers or payment delays.
  • Growing volume without wanting to scale staff proportionally.

When it needs more care

  • Highly variable documents: if every supplier uses a completely different format and the documents are few, the cost of configuring the models might not be amortized.
  • Very low scan quality: heavily degraded documents, irregular handwriting or non-standard fonts can significantly reduce recognition accuracy.
  • No verification process: without a flow that includes human review of doubtful cases, the risk of feeding wrong data into the management system isn't eliminated — only shifted.

The rule of thumb: if an experienced operator takes more than 2 minutes on average to process a document, and the volume exceeds 200 documents a month, the ROI calculation is almost always in favor of automation.

Where to start: a realistic path

Adopting a Document Intelligence system doesn't require a major digital-transformation project. A pragmatic path unfolds over a few weeks, not months, if you start from clear and well-defined goals.

  1. Volume and format analysis: gather a representative sample of the documents you want to automate — at least 50-100 delivery notes from different suppliers. Analyze how many different suppliers there are, which fields always appear, which are variable, and what the average quality of the scans or PDFs is. This phase takes 1-2 days but defines feasibility and the expected ROI.
  2. Model configuration: modern Document Intelligence services let you train custom models with just a few dozen labeled examples. Configuration for a subset of high-volume suppliers typically takes 1-2 weeks, with improvement iterations based on real output.
  3. Pilot on a specific flow: before automating everything, start the system in "supervision" mode: documents are processed by the AI but every result is verified by an operator. This lets you measure real accuracy, identify problematic cases and build trust in the system before reducing the level of supervision.
  4. Extension and optimization: once the pilot is consolidated, extend to other document types and suppliers. Over time, the models improve thanks to operator feedback, and the share of documents reaching the "verified" state without human intervention progressively increases.

The key to success isn't technological: it's organizational. You need an internal owner who takes ownership of the process, defines the quality rules, manages exceptions and drives continuous improvement. Technology is an enabling tool; the process is what determines the result [5].
