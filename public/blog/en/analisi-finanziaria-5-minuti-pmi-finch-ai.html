Numbers alone don't speak

You open the management system, export the data, paste it into Excel. The revenue is there, the costs too, the margin adds up. And yet, when asked "how is the business really doing?", the answer stays vague. Not because the numbers are missing, but because numbers, on their own, say nothing.

It's the paradox that thousands of SMEs live every day: they have ERPs, management systems, accounting software — tools that produce mountains of structured data. But that data stays mute, trapped in tables that no one has the time (or the skills) to interpret in the right context. Is a 12% operating margin good or bad? It depends on the sector, on the trend of recent quarters, on the comparison with competitors. Without context, it's just a number.

The problem isn't the tool. Even those who have moved beyond Excel and work with a modern ERP run into the same barrier: turning raw data into decisions requires time, analytical skills and a holistic view that is often missing in the lean structures of an SME.

The data confirms it. According to the ISTAT "Businesses and ICT" 2025 report, nearly 80% of Italian companies with at least 10 employees reach a basic level of digitization[1]. But "basic level" means having the tools, not knowing how to exploit them. In fact, only 19% of SMEs adopt advanced digital solutions in a structured way, according to the Politecnico di Milano Observatory[2]. The most striking figure? 59% of SMEs report a lack of specialized people to interpret their own data[2].

And then there's the time issue. Who handles financial analysis in an SME with 20-50 employees? Often the owner themselves, or an administrative manager already swamped with deadlines. The result? Strategic analysis is postponed, decisions are made "by feel", and the warning signs in the financials only emerge when it's too late.

What changes with AI applied to financial statements

Artificial intelligence doesn't replace the accountant or the CFO. It does something different and, in some ways, more valuable: it puts the numbers in context, automatically and in real time.

A financial statement isn't a photograph — it's a story. But to read it you need to compare periods, calculate ratios, identify anomalies, cross-reference items. Operations an expert analyst performs in hours, and that AI can perform in minutes. Not because it's smarter, but because it's designed to do exactly this: find patterns, compare, contextualize.

This is not theory. Globally, 59% of corporate finance functions already use at least one AI technology[3]. Gartner predicts that by 2026 this percentage will rise to 90%[4]. And in Italy? AI adoption among companies doubled in a single year: from 8.2% in 2024 to 16.4% in 2025[1]. The signal is clear: those who don't move now will fall behind.

The most relevant aspect for SMEs is that AI applied to finance requires neither data scientists nor complex infrastructure. Modern solutions — like Finch-AI — work directly on the data the company already has: financial statements, invoices, bank statements, exports from the management system. The AI reads them, cross-references them, and returns a clear, understandable picture.

Finch-AI in action: from invoice to insight

Let's see concretely what happens when an SME uses Finch-AI to analyze its financial data. The process is designed to be accessible even to those without advanced financial training.

The numbers that matter: ROI and concrete benefits

Talking about AI is easy. The question every entrepreneur rightly asks is: "how much does it cost me and how much does it return?". The international data is encouraging.

Companies that adopt intelligent automation in financial processes record a median ROI of 150% in the first year of use, with peaks up to 300% for accounts-payable processes[5]. In terms of time, finance professionals save on average 57 minutes a day thanks to AI, time that can be reinvested in strategic activities[6].

But the biggest benefit for an SME isn't the saving itself: it's the ability to decide sooner and better. An analysis that arrives in 5 minutes instead of 5 days radically changes the quality of decisions. It means catching a cash problem before it becomes an emergency, seizing a positive trend while there's still time to invest in it, negotiating with suppliers with fresh data at hand.

In Italy, the most digitally mature companies record about a 6% higher operating margin than the average[1]. And with data analysis on the rise — from 26.6% to 42.7% of companies in the last two years[7] — the message is clear: those who analyze their data perform better.

"But is my company too small for AI?"

It's the most frequent objection. And the answer is no — in fact, it's quite the opposite.

Large companies have dedicated teams of controllers, analysts, data scientists. SMEs don't. They have an administrative manager who does everything, an accountant who sees the numbers after the fact, and an owner who decides based on experience. AI isn't for those who already have an analysis department: it's for those who don't.

Finch-AI is designed exactly for this. It requires no complex integrations, presumes no technical skills, needs no Big Data. It works with the data you already have: the financial statement you send to your accountant, the export from the management system, the invoices in PDF. The goal isn't to turn an SME into a tech multinational, but to give the owner and the CFO the same level of financial awareness that today only large companies have.

The digital gap between large companies and SMEs in AI adoption is widening — from 20 percentage points in 2023 to 37 in 2025[1]. But this isn't fate: it's an opportunity. SMEs that adopt tools like Finch-AI today don't just close a technological gap — they gain a real competitive advantage over competitors who keep flying blind.

💡 No Big Data needed

Finch-AI doesn't need millions of records to work. Even a single annual financial statement contains hundreds of relationships between items that, analyzed in the right context, reveal the company's real health. It's not the quantity of data that matters — it's the quality of the analysis.
