The invisible problem in SMEs

There's a silent paradox affecting thousands of SMEs: they produce enormous amounts of data every day, yet make strategic decisions based on partial, outdated or simply unfindable information. Not because the data is missing. But because no one can see it all together.

Administration works in Excel. Production updates the ERP. Sales manages contacts via email and PDFs. Logistics has its own software. The result? Four photographs of the same elephant, taken from four different angles, that no one manages to combine into a single picture.

This phenomenon — known as information silos — is one of the main brakes on the competitiveness of mid-sized companies. And the problem is that it often isn't recognized as such, because "we've always done it this way" and "all in all it works".

What information silos are and why they form

An information silo is an isolated data store, accessible only to part of the organization and not connected to the other company systems. It's not a conscious choice: it's the natural result of disorderly business growth.

Over time, each department adopts the tools that best fit its immediate needs. Sales starts with Excel, then moves to a CRM. Production uses the factory software. Accounting lives on the ERP. The warehouse has its own inventory system. No one is wrong, and yet the collective result is chaotic.

The most common causes

  • Unplanned organic growth: the company grows and each function acquires its own tools without an overall vision
  • Lack of data governance: no one is responsible for defining how data should flow between systems
  • Technological legacy: old systems that don't talk to modern technologies
  • Resistance to change: departments protect "their" data as a source of power
  • Perceived integration costs: integration seems expensive and complicated, so it's postponed indefinitely

The real cost of data fragmentation

Saying that silos "cost" sounds like an abstraction. But the costs are very concrete and measurable, even if they're rarely attributed to their true cause.

Practical example: the invoicing delay

A manufacturing company with 40 employees issues invoices with an average delay of 12 days from delivery, because shipping data (in the logistics software) has to be transcribed manually into the invoicing system. This delay generates a financial cost estimated at €18,000–25,000 a year, just in interest on working capital. The problem was never attributed to silos, but to "the office's inefficiencies".

Here are the typical costs SMEs suffer without linking the cause to information silos:

  • Wasted person-hours manually transferring data between different systems
  • Transcription errors that create discrepancies between departments and require costly reconciliations
  • Slow decisions because gathering the needed information takes days instead of minutes
  • Missed opportunities because weak signals (sales trends, cost anomalies) aren't detected in time
  • Unreliable reporting with data that varies depending on who extracts it and from which system
  • Audit and compliance made harder and more expensive because data isn't traceable consistently

Excel, ERP, PDF, email: the typical information patchwork

Each tool has its strengths — and its structural limitations when it comes to sharing information with the rest of the organization.

Excel: powerful, but isolated

Excel is the most-used tool in SMEs. It's flexible, familiar and requires no training. But every Excel file is a silo by definition: it lives on someone's desktop, is updated manually, doesn't sync with any other system. When "Maria's file" is the only source of truth for the budget, the company has a structural problem.

ERP: centralized but often underused

The ERP is born to integrate business flows. But in SMEs it's often implemented partially, with unconfigured modules, customizations that make updates impossible, and users who "bypass" the system with their own Excel sheets because the ERP is too rigid. The result is an ERP that manages only part of the processes, while the rest lives outside it.

PDFs and paper documents: "locked" data

Purchase orders, delivery notes, supplier invoices, contracts: an enormous amount of strategic information is trapped in unstructured PDFs or, worse, in physical paper. Extracting this data requires hours of manual work or Document Intelligence technologies that few SMEs have yet adopted.

Email: the worst silo of all

Email is where decisions go to die. Approvals, order changes, informal agreements with suppliers: all buried in inaccessible threads, archived in the personal inbox of someone who may no longer work at the company. Email is the most dangerous silo because no one perceives it as a data-management problem.

The warning signs managers ignore

How do you recognize whether your company suffers from a silo problem? Here are the most common — and often normalized — signs:

  • When you want a report, you have to wait for someone to "prepare" it by gathering data from multiple sources
  • Different departments have different numbers for the same metric (e.g. monthly revenue)
  • New employees struggle to find information and always have to ask the same person
  • Meetings start with 10-15 minutes of "data alignment" before decisions can be discussed
  • You don't know in real time how many open jobs you have, what your current margin is or how much stock you hold
  • You've had at least one case where a customer received conflicting information from two different departments

"We don't have a data problem, we have too much data." This phrase, often heard in SMEs, is exactly the symptom of the problem: it's not the quantity that's the issue, it's accessibility and consistency.

How to escape the silo trap

The good news is that the information-silo problem is solvable — and doesn't always require an expensive technological revolution. SMEs that tackle it successfully follow a gradual, pragmatic approach.

1. Map the data flow

Before buying any technology, you need to understand where the data lives today: which systems exist, who uses them, what data they produce, and where there are transmission "gaps". This mapping takes 1-2 days but is fundamental.

2. Identify the most critical connections

Not everything needs to be integrated at once. You need to identify the data flows that have the greatest impact on operational decisions: typically sales → production → warehouse → invoicing. These are the priorities.

3. Adopt lightweight integration technologies

Today there are tools that let you connect heterogeneous systems without replacing them: integration middleware, API connectors, cloud data-warehouse platforms. Modern solutions are within reach of SMEs too and don't require months of implementation.

4. Use AI to unlock unstructured data

For PDFs, email and paper documents, AI-based Document Intelligence technologies make it possible to automatically extract structured data from any type of document. This is often the fastest starting point with the most immediate ROI.

5. Build a culture of shared data

Technology alone is not enough. A cultural shift is needed: data must be seen as a shared company asset, not the "property" of a department. This requires leadership, training and — above all — concrete examples of how integrated data improves decisions.

The role of AI in breaking down silos

Next-generation AI solutions don't just analyze already-structured data: they're able to read unstructured documents (PDFs, invoice images, emails), extract the relevant data and automatically feed the company systems. This means that even the information assets "locked" in years of paper or digital documents can be recovered and made usable within a reasonable timeframe.

Conclusion

The problem of information silos in SMEs is widespread, underestimated and costly. But its solution doesn't necessarily require large investments or radical transformations: it requires awareness of the problem and a methodical approach to solving it.

The first question to ask isn't "which software should I buy?" but "where does my data live today and who can see it all together?". If the answer is "no one" or "I have to ask three different people", the path to take is clear.

The data is already there. The value is already there. All that's missing is the system to see it.
