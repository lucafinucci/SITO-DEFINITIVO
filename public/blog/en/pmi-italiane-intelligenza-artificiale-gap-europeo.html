The Italian paradox: manufacturing power, digital lag

Italy is Europe's second manufacturing power. Our companies export worldwide, from precision mechanics to food, from fashion to automotive components. Yet, when it comes to artificial-intelligence adoption, the picture changes radically: according to Eurostat 2025 data, only 16.4% of Italian companies with at least 10 employees use AI technologies[1], against a European average of 20%[2]. In Denmark the figure reaches 42%, in Finland 37.8%.

It's a figure that should worry anyone running a business in this country. Not because AI is a trend to chase, but because it's rapidly becoming a structural factor of competitiveness. And the gap, rather than narrowing, is widening.

The Italian artificial-intelligence market reached €1.8 billion in 2025, with 50% growth over the previous year[3]. The numbers suggest dynamism, but they hide a deeply unbalanced distribution: 71% of large companies have started at least one AI project, against just 15% of medium-sized companies and 7% of small ones[3]. In a country where SMEs represent 99% of the business fabric, this asymmetry is a strategic problem, not just a statistic.

Italy's 16.4% does represent a doubling from the 8.2% of 2024[1], a sign that something is moving. But the adoption speed of the Nordic countries — Denmark gained 14.5 percentage points in a single year[2] — makes it clear that without a decisive acceleration the gap will keep growing.

The digital-skills knot

Italy carries a structural deficit in ICT skills. According to the Digital Decade 2025 report, the share of ICT specialists in total employment in Italy is 4%, below the European average[4]. Even more critical is the figure on ICT graduates: Italy stands at 1.5% of total graduates, the lowest value in the entire European Union, against an average of 4.5% and the Estonian peak of 9.6%[4].

Without people able to design, implement and manage AI solutions, even the most motivated companies struggle to start. This deficit feeds a vicious circle: fewer skills available means fewer projects launched, which in turn reduces demand for specialized training.

The widening size gap

ISTAT data shows a worrying trend: the gap in AI usage intensity between large companies and SMEs is widening, not narrowing. It went from about 20 percentage points in 2023 to 25 in 2024, up to 37 points in 2025[1]. Large companies reached an adoption rate of 53.1%, SMEs stop at 15.7%.

 Warning: a gap that's accelerating

In 2023 the AI gap between large companies and SMEs was 20 percentage points. In 2025 it rose to 37. If the trend continues, by 2028 large companies will operate in a completely different ecosystem from SMEs, with deep implications for supply chains and commercial relationships.

Why SMEs fall behind

The reasons for the lag aren't mysterious, but understanding them is the first step to overcoming them. From the analysis of Eurostat, ISTAT and Politecnico di Milano Observatory data, four main barriers emerge.

The skills barrier

Among European companies that considered adopting AI but then didn't proceed, 70.9% point to the lack of relevant skills as the main obstacle[2]. In Italy the figure is even more marked: almost 60% of companies that evaluated AI investments then abandoned them due to a lack of adequate skills[1].

It's not just about data scientists or ML engineers. SMEs often lack people able to translate operational needs into technical specs, manage an automation project, or simply critically evaluate the offers of AI-solution vendors.

Regulatory uncertainty

52.5% of European companies that haven't adopted AI cite the lack of clarity on legal consequences[2]. With the entry into force of the European AI Act and Italy's national AI law (Law 132/2025, in force since October 10, 2025)[5], the regulatory framework is taking shape. But fewer than 30% of European SMEs have taken concrete steps to comply[6], and only 15% of large Italian companies have launched structured compliance projects[3].

The perception of costs

Many entrepreneurs still associate AI with multi-million projects reserved for multinationals. The reality of 2026 is very different: vertical SaaS solutions, pre-trained models and low-code platforms have drastically lowered the entry threshold. Yet the perception of prohibitive costs persists, fueled by industry communication often geared toward large enterprise projects.

The fragmentation of the business fabric

Italy has a very high proportion of micro and small companies, with an average size smaller than its European competitors[7]. Companies with 5-10 employees, often family-run, have organizational structures with no IT manager, let alone someone responsible for AI innovation. The challenge isn't only technological: it's cultural and organizational.

The cost of inaction

If the reasons for the lag are understandable, the consequences of not acting are equally so — and they are increasingly concrete.

The widening productivity gap

Some context: the average European worker today produces only 76% of what a US worker produces, a significant drop from parity thirty years ago[8]. Technological under-investment has been identified as one of the main causes of this decline. For Italian SMEs, which already suffer lower productivity than large companies, ignoring AI means worsening a structural gap.

According to IBM research, two-thirds of EMEA companies that adopted AI report significant productivity gains[9]. McKinsey estimates indicate that annual labor-productivity growth from AI could range between 0.2 and 1.3 percentage points over the next decade[10]. For an economy like Italy's, where productivity growth has been stagnant for thirty years, even 0.5% a year would be a turning point.

Competitiveness in value chains

Italian SMEs are often part of international supply chains. When customers — large companies or foreign distributors — adopt digitized processes and expect equally efficient suppliers, those who don't keep up risk being replaced. Document automation, real-time traceability, the ability to respond quickly to demand changes: these are all requirements that AI makes possible and that markets are starting to take for granted.

The PNRR deadline

Italy allocated €46.8 billion of its National Recovery and Resilience Plan to digital transformation[4]. The plan closes on June 30, 2026, and between January and June of this year the country still has to implement 30% of the targets[11]. After this date, a contraction of public investment in digitization of about 30% is expected[11]. SMEs that don't seize this window of opportunity will find themselves investing with less public support and more accumulated lag.

Concrete use cases for SMEs

AI isn't a monolith. For SMEs, the most accessible and highest-return applications are those that solve concrete operational problems, without requiring organizational revolutions.

Document and administrative automation

The business areas where AI is most frequently adopted by Italian companies are marketing and sales (33.1%), the organization of administrative processes (25.7%) and research and development (20%)[1]. It's no coincidence: document management — invoices, delivery notes, orders, contracts — absorbs time and resources in every SME. AI-based Document Intelligence solutions can extract data from unstructured documents, reduce data-entry errors and speed up accounting and logistics processes.

Demand forecasting and inventory management

For manufacturing and trading SMEs, forecasting demand is a daily challenge. Machine-learning models can analyze sales history, seasonality and external variables to generate more accurate forecasts, reducing both stockouts and excess inventory. It's an area where even relatively small datasets — the kind typical of an SME — produce significant results.

Quality control and predictive maintenance

In manufacturing, computer-vision systems and smart sensors make it possible to detect defects in real time and predict failures before they happen. For a company with 50 employees and one production line, avoiding even a single unexpected machine stop per month can translate into tens of thousands of euros saved.

Customer support and sales

Smart chatbots, automatic request-classification systems, sentiment analysis on feedback: AI applied to the customer relationship is among the fastest-growing areas (+92.6% in marketing and sales versus 2024[1]). The point isn't to replace the human relationship, but to enhance it: respond faster, personalize offers, identify cross-selling opportunities.

Financial analysis and management control

There's an often-underrated area where AI can make a difference in SMEs: financial analysis. Today, in the typical small-to-medium Italian company, the accountant or administrative manager spends hours consolidating data from Excel sheets, checking budget deviations, preparing reports for management. These are repetitive, error-prone, low-value-added activities — exactly the kind of work where artificial intelligence excels.

AI solutions for financial analysis can automate accounting reconciliation, identify cash-flow anomalies, generate budget-vs-actual variance reports and even produce narrative commentary on the most significant deviations. For an SME's financial analyst, this means moving from the manual work of collecting and cleaning data to the strategic interpretation of results: less time "making the numbers add up", more time understanding what those numbers mean for the business.

It's not about replacing the professional, but about amplifying their abilities. An AI system can analyze thousands of transactions in seconds to spot anomalous patterns that a manual check would take days to find. It can automatically compare financial-statement data with industry benchmarks, flagging areas of underperformance. It can generate cash-flow forecasts based on historical data and exogenous variables, supporting critical decisions such as investment planning or trade-credit management.

  From operations to strategy

According to ISTAT data, the organization of administrative processes is the second area of AI adoption in Italian companies (25.7%), with growth of 89.4% versus 2024[1]. The signal is clear: companies adopting AI are using it precisely to transform administrative and financial functions from cost centers into strategic levers.

How to start: a pragmatic approach

The good news is that getting started doesn't require millions of euros or a team of data scientists. It requires method, realistic expectations and the right choice of the first project.

Start from the problem, not the technology

The most common mistake is looking for an application for AI, instead of starting from a real operational problem. Which process absorbs the most time? Where are errors concentrated? What's the activity employees find most repetitive and frustrating? That's where you start.

Choose measurable quick wins

The first AI project must produce visible results within 2-3 months, not 2-3 years. Automating a document process, implementing automatic email classification, assisted report generation: these are all examples of quick wins that build internal trust and justify subsequent investments.

Invest in skills, even minimal ones

In 2025, job postings in Italy requiring AI skills grew by 93%[3]. Not every employee needs to become an expert, but it's essential that management understands what AI can and cannot do. AI-literacy programs for business decision-makers are a very low-cost investment with an enormous strategic return.

Rely on specialized partners

SMEs don't have to do everything in-house. The Italian ecosystem counts over 1,010 companies active in offering AI solutions and 135 specialized startups[3]. Choosing a partner that knows the specific sector, offers scalable solutions and supports the company even after implementation is often more effective — and less risky — than trying to build skills from scratch.

Seize the funding window

With the PNRR in its final phase and digitization calls still active, 2026 represents a unique opportunity. 91% of SMEs using PNRR funds have directed them to digitization[11]. Getting informed about available vouchers, innovation tax credits and regional calls isn't an option: it's an entrepreneurial duty.

The challenge of 2026 isn't adopting AI to follow a trend, but turning widespread adoption into sustainable, measurable and governed change. It takes well-organized data, widespread technical skills and a company culture open to experimentation.
